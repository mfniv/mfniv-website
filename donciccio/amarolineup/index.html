Don Ciccio & Figli

Tasting Smart Cards

Ordered to match your tasting lineup. Use the jump menu when you need to move fast, then scroll the cards naturally during service.

1

Ambrosia

Aperitivo1908Opener
Anchor: turmeric citrus spritz
Core Notes
  • Blood orange
  • Turmeric
  • Cantaloupe
  • Carrot
  • Bittersweet botanicals
What It Feels Like

Bright, refreshing, lightly bittersweet, and easy to place for guests who are new to aperitivi.

Used In

Spritzes first. Also strong as a low-friction aperitivo pour for guests who want something citrusy and approachable.

What It Can Replace

A softer, more nuanced stand-in for Aperol in a spritz build when you want more botanical complexity.

Talk Track

Start the tasting here. This is your easy on-ramp: citrus-led, refreshingly bittersweet, and built for spritz drinking.

2

Amaro delle Sirene

Amaro1931Barrel-aged
Anchor: oak-aged herbal depth
Core Notes
  • Eucalyptus
  • Ripe fruit
  • Licorice
  • Gentian
  • Chicory
  • Chamomile
What It Feels Like

Round, mature, and layered, with a real barrel-aged amaro presence.

Used In

Sipping neat, amaro flights, and higher-complexity stirred drinks where oak and herb depth matter.

What It Can Replace

Think of it as a house answer to bigger traditional amari with oak influence. It can sub for other deep, sipping-style amari when you want less brute force and more elegance.

Talk Track

This is the flagship amaro move in the lineup: barrel-aged, layered, and serious without getting punishing.

3

Donna Rosa Rabarbaro

Aperitivo1896Floral
Anchor: elegant rhubarb rose
Core Notes
  • Rhubarb root
  • Rose petals
  • Vanilla
  • Herbal bitterness
What It Feels Like

Aromatic, lifted, and polished. Floral without turning perfumey.

Used In

Aperitivo pours, floral highballs, and cocktails where you want rhubarb structure instead of blunt citrus bitterness.

What It Can Replace

A more elegant alternative to rhubarb-forward aperitifs or softer red bitter components when you want floral detail.

Talk Track

Present this as the romantic bottle in the lineup: rhubarb backbone, rose lift, and enough bitterness to stay serious.

4

Karkadé

Aperitivo1899Hibiscus-led
Anchor: hibiscus tea with bite
Core Notes
  • Hibiscus
  • Tart floral fruit
  • Medium bitterness
What It Feels Like

Fragrant, bright, and more floral than most guests expect from a bitter aperitivo.

Used In

Tiki-adjacent drinks, gin cocktails, aperitivo builds, and anything that benefits from tart floral lift.

What It Can Replace

A floral-leaning substitute for red bitters when Campari reads too heavy or too blunt.

Talk Track

This is your bridge for guests who like floral tea, berry tartness, or gin drinks but are unsure about amaro.

5

Cerasum

Aperitivo1906Cherry-led
Anchor: tart cherry amaro
Core Notes
  • Three kinds of cherry
  • Sakura blossom
  • Herbal bitterness
What It Feels Like

Bright, tart, and structured enough to work in serious cocktails.

Used In

Spritzes, Negronis, tiki drinks, and refreshing fruit-forward builds.

What It Can Replace

A more bitter, more botanical swap for cherry liqueurs when you want less candy and more architecture.

Talk Track

Frame it as cherry with backbone. It is not dessert cherry. It is cocktail cherry with a bitter spine.

6

Carciofo

Aperitivo1911Savory
Anchor: savory artichoke Negroni
Core Notes
  • Artichoke
  • Cardoon
  • Grapefruit
  • Earthy herbs
What It Feels Like

Vegetal, structured, and savory in a way that reads adult immediately.

Used In

Rum Negronis, Americanos, and savory bitter cocktails.

What It Can Replace

A house alternative to Cynar-style artichoke bitterness, especially when you want something brighter and more citrus-linked.

Talk Track

Sell this to guests who like savory cocktails, vegetal bitterness, or Cynar-adjacent profiles.

7

Luna

Aperitivo1894Bitter-forward
Anchor: dark red Negroni engine
Core Notes
  • Grapefruit
  • Prickly pear
  • Gentian
  • Chicory
  • Rhubarb
  • Vanilla
What It Feels Like

Dark red, multi-layered, and more bitter than the earlier aperitivi in the flight.

Used In

Negroni-inspired cocktails, Boulevardiers, and other bitter-driven stirred drinks.

What It Can Replace

A more nuanced stand-in for Campari or other robust red bitters when you want more layers than just orange-and-bitter.

Talk Track

If someone in the group says they like bitter cocktails, this is where you point and smile.

8

Amaro Tonico Ferro-Kina

Amaro1967Quinine / Tonico
Anchor: iron-toned quinine
Core Notes
  • Bitter lemon
  • China calisaya / cinchona
  • Gentian
  • Iron citrate
What It Feels Like

Assertive, medicinal in the best sense, and tonic-like with a mineral edge.

Used In

Sipping for bitter drinkers, quinine-forward cocktails, and any build where tonic bitterness is the point rather than an accessory.

What It Can Replace

A more complex substitute for quinquina, chinato-adjacent components, or bitter tonic elements in cocktails.

Talk Track

Tell guests this is what happens when tonic bitterness grows up and gets serious.

9

Cinque Bianco

Aperitivo1929Vegan friendly
Anchor: bitter orange + gentian
Core Notes
  • Bitter orange
  • Gentian lutea
  • Herbal bitterness
What It Feels Like

Clean, firm, and classic. A bitter aperitivo without extra ornament.

Used In

Spritzes, Negronis, and straightforward bitter aperitivo cocktails.

What It Can Replace

A white or lighter-color alternative to common bitter aperitivo components when you want gentian and orange without a darker red profile.

Talk Track

Use this as the stripped-down bitter card: citrus, gentian, structure, done right.

10

Amaro Don Fernet

Amaro1915Fernet style
Anchor: mint + dark chocolate punch
Core Notes
  • Fresh mint
  • Dark chocolate
  • Ginger
  • Saffron
What It Feels Like

Sharp, bitter, cooling, and long on the finish. Barrel-aged and built with intent.

Used In

Shots for Fernet people, stirred amaro cocktails, and riffs where minty bitterness needs more detail than standard Fernet.

What It Can Replace

A direct conversation piece against Fernet-Branca style pours, but with no glycerin or menthol and a more custom house identity.

Talk Track

Do not oversell this as generic Fernet. It belongs in that family, but it has its own fingerprint.

11

Limoncello

Liqueur1947Citrus
Anchor: Amalfi lemon oil
Core Notes
  • Fresh lemon peel
  • Sweet citrus
  • Zesty finish
What It Feels Like

Bright, full, and natural. Lemon peel first, not candy first.

Used In

Sipping chilled, citrus cocktails, spritz riffs, and dessert-adjacent drinks.

What It Can Replace

A cleaner, fresher substitute for commercial limoncello or sweet citrus liqueurs when you want real peel character.

Talk Track

Keep the message simple: fresh lemons, no coloring, no stabilizers, and a real lemon finish.

12

Mandarinetto

Liqueur1948Citrus
Anchor: fresh mandarin peel snap
Core Notes
  • Mandarin orange
  • Tangelo
  • Citrus oil
What It Feels Like

Aromatic, juicy, and brighter than standard orange liqueur expectations.

Used In

Margarita-adjacent riffs, citrus cocktails, and any build where orange liqueur should taste fresher.

What It Can Replace

Triple sec, curaçao, or many standard orange liqueurs when you want a fresher natural citrus profile.

Talk Track

This is the easiest practical comparison in the whole lineup: tell people it can step into the orange-liqueur lane, but with more freshness and less generic sweetness.

13

Fico d'India

Liqueur1954Fruit
Anchor: honeyed prickly pear
Core Notes
  • Prickly pear
  • Kiwi-like fruit
  • Honeyed sweetness
  • Crisp finish
What It Feels Like

Exclusive, aromatic, and fruit-led without feeling sticky.

Used In

Mezcal cocktails, pisco drinks, prosecco builds, and fruit-driven aperitivo serves.

What It Can Replace

A more distinctive swap for prickly pear syrups or fruit liqueurs when you want real fruit character plus more elegance.

Talk Track

Guests remember this one because the fruit is unusual. Lean into that and keep the comparison simple: prickly pear, kiwi-like, honeyed, crisp.

14

Finocchietto

Liqueur1954Digestif
Anchor: fresh fennel field
Core Notes
  • Anise
  • Fresh fennel
  • Vegetal herbs
What It Feels Like

Clean, savory, and palate-refreshing. More kitchen herb than candy anise.

Used In

Digestif pours, savory cocktails, and culinary-minded drinks.

What It Can Replace

An herb-fresh alternative to anise liqueurs such as sambuca, anisette, or fennel-driven digestifs when you want less sugar and more freshness.

Talk Track

This one makes sense to cooks immediately. Frame it as a savory digestif, not a candy-shop anise bomb.

15

Nocino

Liqueur1933Walnut digestif
Anchor: green walnut spice cabinet
Core Notes
  • Green walnuts
  • Baking spice
  • Vanilla
  • Ripe berries
What It Feels Like

Dense, semi-sweet, and long on the finish. It reads like holiday memory in liquid form.

Used In

Digestif pours, after-dinner service, spirit-forward stirred drinks, and autumnal cocktails.

What It Can Replace

Classic nocino, walnut liqueur, or certain darker dessert-adjacent modifiers when you want more spice and depth.

Talk Track

Use the San Giovanni harvest story if the group likes tradition. Otherwise just say green walnut, baking spice, and long finish.

16

Cioccolato

Liqueur1971Cocoa
Anchor: bittersweet chocolate orange
Core Notes
  • Three kinds of chocolate
  • Bitter orange
  • Vanilla
  • Spice
What It Feels Like

Velvety, rich, and dessert-like, but still useful in cocktails rather than only after-dinner sipping.

Used In

Bourbon cocktails, dessert drinks, and orange-chocolate riffs.

What It Can Replace

A stronger, more layered substitute for chocolate liqueurs or crème de cacao styles when you want bitter orange and spice built in.

Talk Track

If someone likes bourbon and dessert flavors, this is an easy yes.

17

Concerto

Liqueur1696Coffee / herbal
Anchor: monastery mocha
Core Notes
  • Roasted barley
  • Espresso coffee
  • Licorice
  • Dark chocolate
  • Vanilla
What It Feels Like

Warming, gently sweet, and old-world in a way guests immediately remember.

Used In

After-dinner pours, coffee cocktails, dessert service, and any drink that wants darker roasted depth.

What It Can Replace

A more herbal, more historically textured alternative to coffee liqueur when you want roasted grain and botanicals alongside the coffee note.

Talk Track

Strong closer. It gives you coffee, dark chocolate, roasted grain, and a monk-story frame people actually remember.

Best live-use move: hit category, anchor, two notes, one use case, then move. Keep the long story in reserve unless the room wants it.
